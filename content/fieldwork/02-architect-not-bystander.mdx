Curiosity about what I do eventually leads to glazed eyeballs and a question about what I mean by *compliance-first AI.* The most honest answer I can give is *the cleanup after the next mess we haven't named yet.*

Let me back up.

AI right now is the wild west. Lots of promise. 2024 was the year of prompt engineering — get the right magic words, get the right answer. 2025 was all about *vibe coding,* which doesn't really work. 2026 is transitioning into *agentic coding* — the year of the agent. 2027 is supposed to be the year of audit trails and governance.

The hype cycle has a tell. Each year's label says what last year forgot to do.

We three founders shaped SynapseDx over a lot of conversations. Mike did the coding. The reasons we shipped any of it were practical: we didn't have developers, and we needed the engineers we eventually hired to be writing actual enterprise code. We built things to use ourselves — on the basis that anything good enough to run our own fledgling company is probably good enough for someone else's.

We needed agents to talk to each other. We needed them to talk to source systems and make sense of the data without anyone having to touch ETL again — twenty-five years of it for me, thirty for Mike, fifteen for Dan. Seventy years between us. We all agree it is the worst.

We needed all of it to be approachable from a chat interface that didn't require IT in the loop.

So what does *compliance-first* actually mean?

The honest answer first: I wanted to know what the *fuck* the agents were doing. As much as I love them, I am still unsure — possibly a little distrustful — of what they are up to. I am not the only one. Governments, which is to say *people,* would also like to know what these systems are up to, especially when what they are up to starts affecting markets, economies, and human beings.

The architectural answer is shorter: compliance-first means the system can be proven to do what your regulators — or your architects — said it would do.

The architectural answer is a consequence of the honest one.

Anthropic has *Routines* — focused on the routine itself running well, not on the audit layer around it. Databricks has Agent Bricks. Microsoft says it has governance; what they actually ship is a log of which agents ran which APIs, executed by which identity token. That is observability dressed as audit. When the auditor asks *why a Dynamics CRM record was updated — and who actually decided it should be,* you start a four-month forensic exercise of chasing emails and spreadsheets to reconstruct what the agent thought it was doing. One breadcrumb at a time.

A handful of other vendors are circling the same problem from different angles. None of them are shipping audited trails out of the box yet. (I keep looking.)

Compliance-first is coming. Probably 2027. The market hasn't caught up because the consequences haven't shown up — yet.

The interesting question, in the meantime, is what the architecture has to *let the routines do.*

A compliance-first routine has to be shareable — you should be able to hand it to a colleague and have them run it the same way. It has to be priced — you should see what it costs to run, in real money, every time it runs. It has to be auditable at the moment of action, not retrofitted at the end of a quarter. And it has to gate the source systems, so the only sanctioned way through is the routine itself.

Routines, in other words, with gusto.

The pricing capability deserves its own beat. Nobody can actually tell you what a given business process costs them with AI. The closest anyone gets is old maths — employee time × average process length. Rough then. Rougher now, because the pipeline does not run on humans any more. Nothing is ever that clean. The number that actually matters — per run, in real money, attributed to the workflow — does not exist as routine output anywhere I have seen.

One of the stranger consequences: we are now fast enough that the bottleneck has moved. We are usually the ones waiting on the customer. The hardest question on any project is no longer *"can we ship this in time?"* — it is *"do you know what you actually want?"* Most don't, until they see what they didn't want.

Yes, it risks putting me out of business. We are essentially building the thing that compresses the work I've spent twenty-five years getting good at. I am aware. I have not made my peace with it — I have decided to own it instead. Doing what I always quietly dreamed of: automating myself out of caring about the *how,* and into caring only that it *works.* That it is reliable. Compliant. Architecturally sound. All the things vibe coding skips.

<PullQuote>I'm the architect. I'm not a bystander.</PullQuote>

The thing I find funny — funny in the way you laugh when you don't quite know what else to do — is that the people most critical of this architectural choice are the ones who'd benefit most from it. Lawyers. Compliance officers. Audit teams. They keep choosing the easy thing.

Right now the easy thing is documenting *which model the AI is using.* As if that's going to last six months. The model is the most volatile part of the system. The thing that needs governing is what the system is *allowed* to do — and that resists being documented because actually documenting it would mean actually constraining something.

We built the version that constrains.

None of this lands until someone gets fined. A bank's helpful agent will return Customer A's portfolio summary to Customer B, who pleasantly asked for theirs. The regulator will reach for the section about high-risk AI under Article 14 of the EU AI Act. The fine will run to seven percent of global turnover, and the headlines will write themselves.

Boards will discover compliance-first overnight.

*— maria*
