I just published a piece on LinkedIn called *Compliance-First AI: The Architecture No One Wants*. It will get a polite number of reposts and the occasional thoughtful comment from people who run governance for a living.

This is the version I couldn't publish there.

The same observation, rephrased for people who are not currently being measured on quarterly OKRs.

Most AI policy lives in a SharePoint folder you can't quite find, in a file called FINAL_v12_REALLY_FINAL — which, judging from the filename, has been final for some time now. The lawyer who wrote it has read it twice. The model it was supposedly governing has read it zero times.

This is what we've decided counts as governance.

<PullQuote>Hope is not a control mechanism. A filing cabinet isn't either.</PullQuote>

A regulator asks for traceability. We send a PDF. The PDF describes what the system *should* do. The system, meanwhile, does what the system does. We've mistaken the artefact for the behaviour. Charming.

On LinkedIn I called this an "architectural gap" and made a clean argument about runtime enforcement and Regulatory Adapter Layers. All true. None of it is the funny part.

The funny part is that calling the document "policy" makes everyone in the room feel governed. Then everyone goes back to their desks. The policy stays where it was. The model keeps doing what it does.

I'm not sure what to do with that observation yet. It's sat with me a while.

*— maria*
