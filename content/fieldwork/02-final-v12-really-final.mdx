Somewhere on your shared drive, in a folder you can't quite find, there is a file called FINAL_v12_REALLY_FINAL. It contains an AI policy. The model doesn't read it. The agent doesn't ask for it. The lawyer reaches for it only after something has already happened.

This is supposed to be governance.

Most AI governance I've encountered lives in PDFs and SharePoint folders no system has ever consulted. Some of it lives in OneTrust, on a dashboard glossy enough to satisfy a board pack. None of it is connected to the thing that is about to make a decision in three hundred milliseconds.

I had been thinking about this in the wrong order. The question isn't whether the policy is correct. It's whether the policy is *operative*.

<PullQuote>Hope is not a control mechanism. A filing cabinet isn't either.</PullQuote>

A regulator asks for traceability. We send a PDF. The PDF describes what the system *should* do. The system, meanwhile, does what the system does. We've mistaken the artefact for the behaviour. Charming.

The companies that age well from here won't be the ones with the strongest policies. They'll be the ones whose policies are wired into the runtime — where, when you change a rule once, the entire estate behaves differently next time it runs. Boring from the outside. Bulletproof from the inside.

Strong governance isn't a barrier to innovation. It's the only way innovation survives contact with a regulator who actually reads.

The interesting question isn't whether you have an AI policy. It's whether your AI knows you have one.
